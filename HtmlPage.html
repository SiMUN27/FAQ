Basics

What is the Exchange Visitor Program?

The Exchange Visitor Program (EVP) was created as part of the Cultural Exchange Act of 1961 to allow participants to temporarily reside in the United States and participate in a variety of education or training programs and to promote cultural exchange between the United States and other countries.


The program is grounded in U.S. public diplomacy efforts and its stated purpose is: "to increase mutual understanding between the people of the United States and the people of other countries by means of educational and cultural exchange; to strengthen the ties which unite us with other nations by demonstrating the educational and cultural interests, developments, and achievements of the people of the United States and other nations, and the contributions being made toward a peaceful and more fruitful life for people throughout the world; to promote international cooperation for educational and cultural advancement; and thus to assist in the development of friendly, sympathetic, and peaceful relations between the United States and the other countries of the world."

President John F. Kennedy signs the Act in the Oval Office, White House, Washington, D.C. (21 September 1961.)

How many people actually use the Exchange Visitor Program?

Exchange Visitor Program provides opportunities for around 300,000 foreign visitors from 200 countries and territories per year. For example, 343.800 J-1 Visas were issued in 2017.

Since 2000, more than 6.5 million students from around the world have experienced American culture through the EVP.

Summer Work and Travel participants help local businesses meet seasonal workforce needs during the season. In 2016, participants contributed $509 million to the U.S. Economy.

What is Summer Work and Travel?

Summer Work and Travel (SWT) is only one of the 15 different categories of the J-1 Visa program. However, it is the most famous because it permits students to work and travel throughout the U.S during the summer.

Once they enter the United States, students can stay for up to four months, during which time they may spend up to one month traveling. When the program ends, students are expected to return home and continue their studies.

What is a J-1 Visa?

A J-1 is a visa issued by the US Embassy or Consulate in your home country. It is a sticker on a page of your passport which allows you to enter the U.S. when you present it with your DS2019 Form at the border.

There is no fixed cap on the number of J-1 visas available annually, but each year the Department of State allocates a pre-set number of Certificates of Eligibility (Form DS 2019) to program sponsors to issue to potential participants across the country.

The J-1 visa is governed by specific regulatory requirements. For example:

  • The minimal and the maximal duration of stay are determined by the specific J-1 category under which an exchange visitor is admitted into the United States.
  • Participants may apply for a Social Security number (SSN). They must pay taxes on any compensation they receive, but are exempt from paying Federal Insurance Contributions Act (FICA) taxes (for Medicare and Social Security).
  • J-1 visa holders must obtain insurance to cover sickness and accidents.
  • The designated sponsors supervise the application process and are the main point of contact throughout the exchange program process.
  • Participants have to check-in every 30 days to maintain active status on the program.

What is a program Sponsor?

Unlike many other countries, the U.S. authorities have delegated part of the visa procedure to organizations that act as a "legal sponsor". These can be viewed as an extension of the U.S. Embassy and are responsible for implementing programs in accordance with all U.S. regulations. You must work with a legal sponsor without exception as the U.S. embassies or consulates only issue the J visa after presenting the DS-2019.

Sponsor will also support and monitor exchange visitors during their stay in the United States. During the program, participants can contact their sponsor if they have any questions or in a case of emergency. Sponsors charge participants program fees. Fees vary from sponsor to sponsor. Participants are encouraged to obtain a breakdown of all costs and fees from multiple sponsors before committing to a program.

Requirements

Are there any requirements I should know about?

Yes, there are a few requirements applicants need to meet for the exchange visitor J-1 Visa. The consular officer will determine whether you qualify for the visa. He will look for the following:

  • That you are older than 18 years old.
  • That you are currently enrolled in college.
  • You must account for a processing time of at least 8 weeks.
  • That you plan to remain in the U.S. for a temporary, specific, limited period of time.
  • That you are able to speak basic English.
  • Evidence of funds to cover expenses in the United States.
  • Evidence of compelling social and economic ties in your home country which will ensure that you return home after the end of the exchange program.

It is worth noting that these requirements are harder to meet in some countries. This depends on a number of applicants from your country that choose to illegally remain in the U.S after the program ends. If the percentage is significant, application process is probably going to be more demanding.

Other specific requirements are tied to the program category that you choose. For example, Au pair category has a lot more regulations than Summer Work and Travel.

If you find yourself in one of these categories, it is probably the right thing for you:

  • If you love meeting new people and expanding your network.
  • If you want to improve your English language skills.
  • If you are ready to have an adventure this summer.
  • If you want to learn more about the United States.
  • If you are bored and not afraid of the unknown.

How much would it cost?

Firstly, to answer the question directly: The average worldwide fee for SWT program participation is $1,533. However, in 2017 these program fees amount varied widely from $300 in France to $3,500 in Italy.

There is some risk of losing money in case your application fails:
The U.S. Consulates charge a non-refundable consulate fee for each visa processing. The current fee is $160.00 USD and is due before the interview.
Since September 1, 2004, an additional fee has been implemented, the Student and Exchange Visitor Information System (SEVIS.) fee of $180.00 USD. Fees for the consulate and for the SEVIS registration cannot be compensated. If your visa is rejected by the US Embassy, you can recover part of the visa costs under some conditions.

We encourage you to refer to your sponsor for accurate program costs. Information found here is for informational purposes only.

When is the right time to apply for exchange visa?

From our experience, it takes about 8-10 weeks to apply for a visa. However, this timeframe depends on the cooperation from your host company as well as how fast you submit all of your required documents. Therefore, to avoid any delays, we advise you to start your visa application as early as possible.

Do i need insurance?

Yes. All interns and their companions (J-Visa) must be insured for the duration of the internship in accordance with the requirements of the U.S. Department of State. Your sponsor should have a couple of insurance options available. Choose the one that suits your needs.

The best practice is to schedule a medical and dental exam before the trip. Always minimize potential health problems while in the U.S. since treatment is very expensive!

What is an orientation meeting?

This is a meeting organised by your sponsor before the program starts. There you will find information on:

  • Life and customs in the United States.
  • Local community resources (e.g., public transportation, medical centers, schools, libraries recreation centers, and banks) to the extent possible.
  • Available health care, emergency assistance, and insurance coverage.
  • A description of the program in which the exchange visitor is participating.
  • Rules that the exchange visitors are required to follow.
  • Sponsor address, telephone number and emergency contact.
It is also useful to meet other participants and find out their motives for applying. Maybe you can spend your grace period in the United States with some of them?

Specifics

How about travel arrangements during the application process?

Generally, it is not a good practice to make any binding travel arrangements like booking flights or accommodations during the application process. No expenses can be refunded if you get your visa denied. Same applies to the interview process at the consulate. We highly advise you to wait until your DS-2019 has been issued or your visa has been approved prior to making any travel plans.

Do I have to pay taxes while working in the U.S.?

Yes, you are responsible for paying U.S. income tax on every paycheck. These taxes should be automatically withheld from your paycheck. In other words, paid directly to the government by your employer.

As a J-1 program participant, there are some taxes that you must pay and others that you do not:

Taxes that you must pay:

  • Federal Income Tax
  • State Income Tax
  • Local Income Tax

Taxes that you do not have to pay:

  • Social Security & Medicare Tax (FICA)
  • Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA)

It is very important to make sure you are not paying unnecessary taxes.
Simply look at the first check and make sure you are not paying FICA or FUTA. Otherwise, warn your employer as he is responsible for tax deductions.

Can I file a tax refund?

At the end of the year, you are eligible to get back some or all of the taxes you paid. As a J-1 visa holder, you are required to submit a tax return to the Internal Revenue Service (IRS), in the calendar year after the program. In this case, it is quite possible that the return amount is not insignificant.

Your sponsor will probably offer to file a tax return for you. This service usually costs extra. There is a free alternative: you can file it yourself...if you are not bored by forms, definitions and instructions. In case there is enough interest, we will make a full tax return guide!

Who should I contact if I have any problems in the U.S.?

If you have any problems, contact your sponsor.

What are my obligations during the program?

During your stay in the United States, you must check in monthly with your sponsor to confirm your visa status. Since the program is based on cultural exchange, you will have to explain how you interact with the locals and which events you are attending.

You are also obliged to inform your sponsor of any changes throughout your stay. For example, if you change your location, quit your job or find a second job.

What is the 30-Day Grace Period?

As J-1 exchange visitor, you are permitted to remain in the country up to 30 days past the end date of your Form DS-2019. This gives you time for traveling and to prepare for departure.

During the grace period, you cannot expect to leave the USA and then return to the USA with your J-1 Visa because that visa expires on the “end date” listed on the DS2019. Because of this, we recommend that participants desiring to travel outside the U.S. during their grace periods (e.g. to Canada, Mexico…) do so only at the end of their grace, and only if such participants have departure flights scheduled from outside the U.S. directly home, not via the U.S for home.

Can I apply for a J-1 Visa more than once?

Yes. The J-1 visa for interns and trainees can be applied for multiple times. However, the certain requirement apply:

it is possible as long as you are officially enrolled as a student. However, you must return to your home country after your J-1 stay in the U.S. and a certain level of progress in your career is evident like completing another semester.

For a trainee however, a 2-year regulation applies. If you wish to apply for another J-1 Trainee visa, you must wait a period of at least two years between two visas. The same applies if you already had a J-1 Intern visa and want to apply for the J-1 Trainee visa.

Terminology

What is SEVIS?

SEVIS stands for Student and Exchange Visitor Information System. SEVIS is a United States Government computerised system that collects and manages data about foreign students and exchange visitors during their stay, including Work & Travel participants. SEVIS is designed to help foreign visitors follow the terms of their visas.

What is DS-2019 form?

The Form DS-2019 or "Certificate of Eligibility for Exchange Visitor (J-1) Status" is the basic document used in the administration of the exchange visitor program that can only be produced through SEVIS. It permits a prospective exchange visitor to seek an interview at a U.S. embassy or consulate in order to obtain a J visa to enter the United States. document required to support an application for an exchange visitor visa (J-1). It is a 2-page document. Foreign nationals are required to sign page one of the form agreeing to the instructions and certification language relating to participation in the exchange program found on page two. No blank SEVIS forms exist. Each Form DS-2019 is printed with a unique identifier known as a “SEVIS ID number” in the top right-hand corner, which consists of an “alpha” character (N) and up to 11 numerical characters (E.g. N0002123457).

The Form DS-2019:

  • Identifies the participant (block 1).
  • Identifies the sponsor (block 2 and block 7, which contain the name, signature and telephone number/address of the sponsor’s responsible officer).
  • Provides a brief description of the program activity to be completed by the participant (block 2).
  • Identifies the beginning and end dates of the program (block 3) and the program category (block 4).
  • Provides a breakout of the total estimated financial support to be provided to the participant during his or her program (block 5).

What is I-94 card?

When international students travel to the United States, they receive a Form I-94, “Arrival/Departure Record” , at their port of entry. International students who enter the country at an air or sea port are issued an electronic Form I-94. You will need to print a copy to prove your legal visitor status in the United States and apply for Social Security Number. You can retrieve a copy online.

To obtain your Form I-94 record, go to CBP's Form I-94 Web page and agree to the terms listed on the page. You will be directed to the Form I-94 information page. Enter your name, date of birth and passport information to retrieve your Form I-94. The Web page gives you the option to “Get Most Recent I-94” or “Get Travel History.” If you select “Get Most Recent I-94,” the Web page will display your most recent Form I-94, which includes an I-94 number, most recent date of entry, class of admission and admit until date. You can print this page if needed. If you select “Get Travel History,” you can access your last five years of travel history on the Form I-94. You can also print this information. If you have additional questions about the Form I-94, select the I-94 FAQ tab on the Form I-94 Web page.

What is SSN?

The social security number (SSN) is a very important in the US as it serves as a universal identification of individuals Without SSN you won't be able to open a bank account, apply for a driver’s license or file your tax return.

You have to apply for SSN personally and:

  • Present your passport.
  • I-94 form.
  • DS-2019 form.
  • and the ‘Employer Letter’.

The nearest office can be found on the official Social Security website.
It is worth noting that if you already have a SSN, you do not need to get another one. A Social Security Number is for life.

On average it takes applicants between 3-5 weeks to receive their Social Security number. However, employer can legally hire you if you have proof that you have already applied for your SSN.

What is W2 form?

Your W-2 form contains information such as the total income you were paid during the year and how much of that income has been withheld from your paycheck. Host organizations have a legal responsibility to provide everyone who is on the company’s payroll with W-2 forms by the deadline of January 31 each year. If you do not receive your W-2 form by this date, you should contact your host company immediately.

When filing taxes, it is important that you possess your W-2 form, because most of the information that is requested on your income tax return is located on the W-2.

What is W4 form?

W-4 form is a tax form completed by an employee to indicate his or her tax situation (exemptions, status, etc.) to the employer. The W-4 form tells the employer the correct amount of tax to withhold from an employee's paycheck. The more deductions you take, the smaller your paycheck, but the larger your potential tax refund at the end of the year. It is a relatively simple concept, but the rules about what you can deduct and what you cannot deduct, whether you should deduct more or less income, and how changes in your pay rate affect deductions are complex.

Since there are many factors to consider, your sponsor will inform you about filling out W4 forms. Make sure to follow their instructions!

Can you explain all of this, but simpler?

Sure!

  • SEVIS - big database full of information about all the exchange visitors.
  • DS-2019 - a form you will need in order to obtain your visa.
  • I-94 - a card that shows dates of entering and leaving the United States.
  • SSN - a unique identifying number in the U.S. It is important! Get it as soon as you can when you arrive.
  • W2 - a form you will need to file your tax return. It should arrive in your mail in January.
  • W4 - a form where you will state which taxes you are paying. You fill it out when you start working. Listen to your sponsor so you know how.

Tips and Tricks

How do i compare offers from different sponsors?
It is always a good idea to consider your options when it comes to choosing your sponsor. Here is a guide to help you determine which sponsor to pick:
  • How much experience does the agency have?
  • What is the total price for the entire process?
  • How much guidance will they provide in filling out the application forms? How much will your host company be involved in this process?
  • Is the insurance optional or a mandatory part of the program? What are the benefits of the specific insurance plan? What are the additional costs per incident/illness?
  • Will you be able to book a flight to the U.S. yourself?
  • How many documents and proofs do you have to provide?
  • How bureaucratic is the program? I.e. number of reference letters required, how does the submission of mandatory documents affect the processing period?
  • How long is the processing time?
What if I want to earn a lot of money?
If money is what you want, this is what you need to do:
  • Aim for wealthier parts of the U.S., like Nantucket.
  • Look for jobs that include tips. Most wanted positions are those of servers and bussers. Have in mind that these positions require somewhat advanced English language proficiency.
  • Try to arrive to the U.S earlier than other participants. You will have more job positions to choose from.
  • Fill your schedule with second and/or third jobs.
  • Try to find a cheap accommodation. This won't be easy if you spend your summer in a tourist-heavy areas.
  • Job that offers free lunch is a good deal so you don't waste your time and money.
How to book the cheapest flight to the U.S.?

Start frequently visiting websites like:

  • kiwi.com
  • skyscanner.net
  • matrix.com
Get used to seeing flight prices change and try to figure out when they are the cheapest. Once you obtain your visa you will know what to look for and hopefully, book your flight at a good price!

Should I prepare for the interview at the American consulate?

There are some things to keep in mind.
The interview itself will probably take just a few minutes. do not take unnecessary documents or electronic devices. Please remember that you are not going to the U.S. to work, rather participate in a cultural exchange. Answer all of their questions honestly and stay calm.

What do I have to do before leaving for the USA?

  • Make copies of all the documents, including the passport.
  • You should know your first address in the US as you must specify this on entry.
  • Inform yourself about the process of your insurance in case of illness and accident.
  • If you are taking regular medications, please take an English-language note from your doctor and the medication in the original packaging. Find out how you can get these drugs in the US.
  • Check if your smartphone is contract-bound. Research where are the best offers for your new SIM card.
  • Don't pack too much stuff. Leave some room if you are planning to buy something in the United States.
  • Read stories and experiences from past participants. Realise that sometimes things do not go as planned.
  • Don't forget to take some money to cover your expenses before your first paycheck.
